## 1. Disease Overview & Pathophysiology

1. **Kasperkiewicz M, Ellebrecht CT, Takahashi H, et al.**  
   Pemphigus. *Nat Rev Dis Primers.* 2017;3:17026.  
   → Comprehensive review: disease classification, autoantibody biology, clinical features, therapies.  
   <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28480895>

2. **Bystryn JC, Rudolph JL.**  
   Pemphigus. *Lancet.* 2005;366(9479):61–73.  
   → Landmark review; epidemiology, pathogenesis, and management of pemphigus.  
   <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15993231>

3. **Amagai M, Klaus-Kovtun V, Stanley JR.**  
   Autoantibodies against a novel epithelial cadherin in pemphigus vulgaris, a disease of cell adhesion. *Cell.* 1991;67(5):869–877.  
   → First demonstration that anti-Dsg3 IgG causes acantholysis; milestone reference.  
   <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1720352>

4. **Amagai M, Koch PJ, Nishikawa T, Stanley JR.**  
   Pemphigus vulgaris antigen (desmoglein 3) is localized in the lower epidermis, the site of blister formation in patients. *J Invest Dermatol.* 1996;106(2):351–355.  
   → Dsg3 distribution explains mucosal-predominant blistering in oral PV.  
   <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8601739>

5. **Mahoney MG, Wang Z, Rothenberger K, et al.**  
   Explanation for the clinical and microscopic localization of lesions in pemphigus foliaceus and vulgaris. *J Clin Invest.* 1999;103(4):461–468.  
   → Dsg compensation theory: Dsg1+Dsg3 coexpression prevents acantholysis in deeper epidermis.  
   <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10021453>
